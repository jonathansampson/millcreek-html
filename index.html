Week 01 | Syntax, and Tags

In our very first class, we took a few minutes to discuss syntax, and what it was. Syntax is what we can write, and where/how it should be written. To help us understand this a little better, we looked at the following example:

howaRE?you

In the above example, we have a question. Unfortunately, the question isn't very easy to read, or understand; this is because we didn't obey the syntax rules of a sentence. There are a few things we can do to improve the example though:

  • Sentences begin with a capital letter
  • Words in a sentence should be seperated by spaces
  • Individual letters shouldn't be capitalized, generally speaking
  • Question-marks should appear at the end of the statement; not near the middle

When we follow the above rules, we can see how the statement is easier to read, and understand:

How are you?

Today we talked about HTML tags; two examples of HTML tags are <p> and <b>. These are opening tags. To stop the effect of an opening tag, we use closing tags. A closing tag looks very similar to an opening tag, only it has a / immediately inside: </p> and </b>.

<p>This is paragraph with a <b>bold</b> word!</p>

This is a paragraph with a bold word!

The other thing we covered today was lists. There are two types of lists: ordered, and unordered.

Ordered List

  1. Put your socks on
  2. Put your shoes on

Unordered List

  • Brush your teeth
  • Brush your hair

In unordered lists, it the order doesn't matter. You can brush your hair first, or you can brush your teeth first.

In ordered lists, the order does matter. You cannot put your shoes on, and then put your socks on ðŸ™‚

The HTML for both is pretty straight-forward:

<p>Ordered List</p> <ol> <li>Put your socks on</li> <li>Put your shoes on</li> </ol> <p>Unordered List</p> <ul> <li>Brush your teeth</li> <li>Brush your hair</li> </ul>

Ordered lists are created with the <ol> tag.

Unordered lists are created with the <ul> tag.
